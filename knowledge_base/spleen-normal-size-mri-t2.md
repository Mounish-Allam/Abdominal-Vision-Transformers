# Spleen — Normal Size and Appearance on T2-Weighted MRI

Normal spleen length is commonly cited as up to roughly 12-13 cm along its
long axis in an average adult, though normal size varies with patient height,
body habitus, and measurement technique. Splenomegaly (an enlarged spleen) is
a recognized clinical finding, but — as with the other organs in this
project — a single 2D axial slice's coverage percentage is only a partial,
local indicator and cannot by itself confirm or exclude splenomegaly, which is
properly assessed from the full 3D volume or a dedicated long-axis measurement.

On T2-weighted sequences, normal splenic parenchyma is relatively homogeneous
and, like the liver and kidneys, benefits from fat suppression (T2-SPIR) for
improved contrast against adjacent retroperitoneal and perisplenic fat. Because
the spleen is the smallest of the four segmented organs, small absolute
measurement errors translate into larger relative (percentage) differences
than they would for the liver, which is a useful context note when interpreting
per-organ confidence or coverage statistics for the spleen specifically.
