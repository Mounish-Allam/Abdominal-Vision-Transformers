# Radiology Report Structure — General Conventions

Radiology reports conventionally separate content into a small number of
distinct sections, each serving a different purpose:

- **Technique** — a brief statement of what was imaged and how (e.g. modality,
  sequence type, region covered). This grounds the reader in what data the
  report is based on before any interpretation is given.
- **Findings** — an objective, structured description of what is observed,
  typically organized by organ or region. Findings should describe what is
  measured or seen (e.g. presence, relative size, signal characteristics,
  symmetry) without yet drawing a diagnostic conclusion.
- **Impression** — a concise summary interpretation, distilling the findings
  into the most clinically relevant takeaways, and explicitly noting anything
  that could not be assessed with confidence or that would benefit from
  further review or correlation.

A well-structured report keeps these sections distinct: findings should be
traceable back to specific measurements or observations, and the impression
should not introduce new claims that aren't supported by the findings section.
This separation is part of what makes a report auditable — a reader can check
whether the impression's conclusions are actually backed by the findings
listed above it, rather than taking the summary purely on faith.
