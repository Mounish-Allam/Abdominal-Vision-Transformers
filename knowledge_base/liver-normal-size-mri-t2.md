# Liver — Normal Size and Appearance on T2-Weighted MRI

Craniocaudal (top-to-bottom) liver span is commonly cited as roughly 10-15 cm
along the mid-clavicular line in an average adult, though this varies with
patient size, body habitus, and respiratory phase at the time of scanning.
Because craniocaudal span is a 3D measurement, it cannot be read directly off
a single 2D axial slice — an individual slice's organ area only reflects
coverage at that slice level, not the organ's total length or volume.

On T2-weighted sequences, healthy liver parenchyma has intermediate-to-low
signal intensity relative to fluid-filled structures (which appear bright).
With fat suppression (as in T2-SPIR), the background fat signal is reduced,
which improves contrast between the liver and surrounding fat and makes organ
boundaries easier to delineate for both manual reading and automated
segmentation. A liver that is markedly enlarged (hepatomegaly) or shows an
irregular contour on imaging warrants clinical correlation; single-slice
segmentation coverage alone cannot establish or rule out hepatomegaly.
