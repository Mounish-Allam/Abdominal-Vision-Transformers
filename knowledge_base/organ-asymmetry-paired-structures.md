# Paired Organ Asymmetry — Kidneys

Of the four organs in this project, only the kidneys are a paired, bilaterally
symmetric structure (the liver and spleen are single, unpaired organs, so
left/right comparison does not apply to them). Because kidneys are paired,
some degree of left-right difference in per-slice coverage or measured size is
expected and normal, not necessarily a sign of a problem.

Contributing factors to normal kidney asymmetry include:

- The right kidney is typically displaced slightly downward relative to the
  left kidney due to the liver occupying space above it, so at a given axial
  slice level the two kidneys may show different amounts of tissue simply due
  to this vertical offset.
- Modest individual anatomical variation in kidney size exists between
  people, and can also exist between a person's own two kidneys, generally
  within a small percentage of each other.

A rough guideline used informally in this project: a left/right coverage
difference under roughly 30% of the larger value is treated as within
plausible normal variation, while a larger difference is flagged as
noteworthy and worth mentioning for human review, since it could reflect a
true asymmetry, a slice-level artifact, or a segmentation error, all of which
are better resolved by a human reader than assumed away.
