# Kidneys — Normal Size and Symmetry on T2-Weighted MRI

Adult kidney length is commonly cited in the range of roughly 9-13 cm,
measured along the long axis in coronal or sagittal planes; width and
thickness are each smaller than length, giving the kidney its characteristic
bean shape. As with any single-organ measurement, a single axial slice only
captures a cross-section at that level, not the full long-axis length, so
per-slice area/coverage should be interpreted as a local snapshot rather than
a whole-organ size measurement.

The kidneys are one of the few paired organs where a meaningful degree of
left-right size difference is still considered normal — differences up to
roughly 1-1.5 cm in length, or a modest percentage difference in per-slice
area, are commonly within normal anatomical variation, partly because the
right kidney is displaced slightly downward by the liver. Substantially larger
asymmetries, or a unilateral difference paired with a shape or signal
abnormality, are more suggestive of a true finding requiring clinical review.

On T2-weighted imaging, normal renal parenchyma shows a corticomedullary
signal pattern (cortex versus medulla contrast), and fat suppression (T2-SPIR)
improves the contrast between kidney tissue and surrounding perinephric fat.
