# Kidneys — Anatomy Overview

The kidneys are a pair of bean-shaped organs located in the retroperitoneum,
one on each side of the spine, roughly at the level of the lower ribs and
upper lumbar vertebrae. Each kidney filters blood — together processing on
the order of half a cup of blood per minute — removing waste products and
excess fluid, which are excreted as urine, while helping regulate fluid
balance, electrolytes, and blood pressure via hormone production.

Internally, each kidney contains roughly a million nephrons, each with a
glomerulus (the filtering unit) and a system of tubules that reclaim needed
water and solutes before urine passes to the ureter. Because the right kidney
sits just below the liver, it is typically positioned slightly lower than the
left kidney in most people — a normal anatomical asymmetry, not a pathological
finding. On cross-sectional imaging, the kidneys appear as two separate,
roughly symmetric organs; substantial size or signal differences between the
two beyond normal variation can be a reason for closer review.
