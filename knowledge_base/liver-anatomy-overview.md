# Liver — Anatomy Overview

The liver is the largest solid organ in the abdomen, situated in the upper right
portion of the abdominal cavity, mostly tucked under the right side of the rib
cage and diaphragm. It spans across the midline, with a large right lobe and a
smaller left lobe, separated internally by connective tissue and blood vessel
boundaries rather than a visible surface groove in most imaging planes.

Functionally, the liver filters blood arriving from the digestive tract,
metabolizes nutrients and drugs, produces bile for fat digestion, synthesizes
plasma proteins, and stores glycogen and certain vitamins. Because of this
central metabolic role, it receives a dual blood supply: the hepatic artery
(oxygen-rich blood) and the portal vein (nutrient-rich blood from the gut).

On cross-sectional imaging, the liver is a single unpaired organ occupying a
large fraction of the upper abdomen, so it typically shows the largest
coverage percentage of any organ segmented on a single axial abdominal slice.
Its texture is normally homogeneous; focal variation or unexpected contour
changes are more likely to reflect pathology, imaging artifact, or slice
positioning than true anatomical variation.
